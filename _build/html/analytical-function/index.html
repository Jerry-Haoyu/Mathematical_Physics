Real Derivative and Complex Derivative¶

A complex function f:C→Cf:\mathbb{C}\to \mathbb{C}f:C→C resembles, more or less, functions like R2→R2\mathbb{R}^2\to \mathbb{R}^2R2→R2. For instance, the complex exponential f(z)=exp⁡(z)f(z)=\exp(z)f(z)=exp(z) would be more or less similar to

Difference 2 : Mixing Of Components

To make it worse, even if we pretend such notation is sensible, they are still different. The limit of the real part lim⁡Δz→0u(z+Δz)−u(z)Δz\lim_{\Delta z\to 0}\frac{u(z+\Delta z)-u(z)}{\Delta z}limΔz→0​Δzu(z+Δz)−u(z)​(which we hoped to correspond to ∂f1∂(x,y)\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial (x,y)}∂(x,y)∂f1​​) has a complex part and similarily the limit of the complex part also has a real part! This means the components are naturally mixing.

So the real difference is that complex number should be viewed as a whole where division is well-defined. One might ask, doesn’t the seem problem exists for the real analog? It’s quite obvious the answer is no, since we never compute a single limit of a finite difference.

In a way, C→C\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}C→C is like R→R\mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R}R→R, we define the derivative to be the limit of finite difference. Yet the limits

Cauchy-Riemann Condition¶

Expanding (9), we would get:

∂u∂x+i∂v∂x=−∂u∂yi+∂v∂y\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+i\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}i + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}∂x∂u​+i∂x∂v​=−∂y∂u​i+∂y∂v​
(12)

Note that this equation essentially unwind the “mixing of components” we mentioned in Difference 2 : Mixing Of Components. Hence we get:

∂u∂x=∂v∂y∂v∂x=−∂u∂y\boxed{\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \qquad \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}}∂x∂u​=∂y∂v​∂x∂v​=−∂y∂u​​
(13)

This is known as the Cauchy-Riemann Condition(C-R condition).

Let’s recall what had happened so far, we discussed the idea of complex derivative being a limit of finite difference leading to computing limits of the form R2→R\mathbb{R}^2\to \mathbb{R}R2→R(two input, one output) which puts additional restriction on differentiability. To be more specific, in order for these limit to exists, the value of the limit must be invariant of directions approaching the point of interest x0,y0x_0, y_0x0​,y0​. Considering the specific direction along the real and complex axis we get the so-called CR condition.

We have effectively shown that CR condition is necessary for complex differentiability. It turns out however, that CR condition is also sufficient.
